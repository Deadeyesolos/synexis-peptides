The Science of HPLC Testing and Mass Spectrometry

14 Mar 2026 4 Min Read

Verifying Purity

In-vitro research demands compounds of the highest purity to yield definitive, replicable data. Impurities, heavy metals, or fragmented amino acid sequences introduced during the synthesis process can trigger cascade artifacts in a cellular model, entirely invalidating an experiment.

At Synexis Peptides, every batch of synthesized protein undergoes rigorous third-party analytical testing prior to being cleared for distribution to the research community.

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

HPLC is an advanced analytical technique used to separate, identify, and quantify the components within a mixture. During testing, a peptide sample is dissolved in a solvent and forced through a specific column under high pressure.

  • Because varying molecular structures travel through the column at different speeds, the primary compound separates completely from any synthesis byproducts or impurities.
  • A UV detector logs the concentration of the molecules as they exit the column, plotting the data onto a chromatogram.
  • The purity percentage (>99%) is calculated by comparing the area of the primary peptide peak to the total area of all peaks combined.

Mass Spectrometry (MS)

While HPLC confirms that the compound is pure, Mass Spectrometry is necessary to confirm that the pure compound is, in fact, the correct peptide sequence.

The pure eluent from the HPLC process is ionized and fired through a magnetic field. By analyzing the mass-to-charge ratio of the ions, the exact molecular weight of the sequence is verified. For example, if MS confirms a sample bears a molecular weight of 1419.55 g/mol, the lab can definitively verify the sequence identity as BPC-157.
