Peptide Storage Guidelines & Reconstitution Protocols

14 Mar 2026 6 Min Read

Storage of Lyophilized Powders

Synthetic peptides are highly susceptible to chemical degradation, including hydrolysis, oxidation, and deamidation. Upon arrival, lyophilized (freeze-dried) compounds must be stored to mitigate these risks prior to experimentation.

  • Short-term storage (up to 4 weeks): Can be stored refrigerated at 2°C - 8°C. Ensure vials are tightly sealed to prevent moisture absorption.
  • Long-term storage (up to 24 months): Transfer immediately to a standalone freezer maintaining -20°C or colder. Avoid "frost-free" freezers as their autonomous thaw cycles introduce damaging temperature fluctuations.
  • Protection from light: UV radiation accelerates amino acid oxidation. Keep vials inside dark, sealed containers.

Reconstitution Protocols

When preparing a peptide for in-vitro analysis, the reconstitution process must be performed cleanly and gently to avoid shearing the delicate amino acid bonds.

  1. Allow the lyophilized vial to reach room temperature before opening. This prevents atmospheric moisture from condensing inside the cold vial.
  2. Swab the rubber stopper of both the peptide vial and the diluent (e.g., Bacteriostatic Water) with 70% isopropyl alcohol.
  3. Using a sterile syringe, draw the calculated volume of diluent. (Use our Peptide Calculator to determine accurate dosing).
  4. Inject the diluent slowly down the inner wall of the peptide vial. Do not blast the delicate powder directly with the liquid stream.
  5. Do not shake aggressively. Roll the vial gently between your fingers or swirl it lightly until the solution is completely clear and dissolved.

Post-Reconstitution Storage

Once a peptide is synthesized into liquid form, its degradation clock accelerates significantly. Reconstituted solutions should be used for research purposes within 14 to 30 days.

Crucial Rule: Reconstituted peptides must be stored tightly sealed in a refrigerator (2°C - 8°C). Never re-freeze a reconstituted peptide solution, as the formation of ice crystals fundamentally destroys the molecular lattice of the peptide sequence, rendering the sample useless for accurate analysis.
